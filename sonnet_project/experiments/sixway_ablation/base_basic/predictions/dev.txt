5
Thou blind fool, Love, what dost thou to mine eyes
That they behold and see not what they see?
They know what beauty is, see where it lies,
but do their sight best.
But that which others call death's nature a thousand ages'
residence;
Shall each one of them take life with him when

And bring his good fortune back again in the dead
tomb.
To those things once so dear are reaped by loss
alone;
Doth all our new acquaintance be like this pale crown
worn up till now?

When my love swears that she is made of truth
I do believe her though I know she lies,
That she might think me some untutored youth,
So couldst they not use to tell a lie
Yet their tongues teach what in thy eyes was thought
most false.
Thus did say mine beloved mistress fell dead;
Such are all those who listen with closed mouths,
To give such creeds for others' sins as from which
nothing can be derived.
But now alas! if you would keep silent
In us your decease may devise no mirth but wretched
tongue
Wherece so many faults thou shalt find and read therein.

9
In faith, I do not love thee with mine eyes,
For they in thee a thousand errors note;
But 'tis my heart that loves what they despise,
And gives of myself to them from their hurt.
But although these beauties are among many things seen,
Mine hatred for thy goodness has taken on several occasions;
Thus when thou art ill dost hear praise be sung;
And after having despised all virtue and beauty alike,
Then thine tongue submits you then even farther.
So why shouldn't such tastes bring me pleasure?
For it is through right malice which by nature bears
down;
All help the weak but those who give themselves up
so well.


10
