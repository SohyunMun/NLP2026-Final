Those lips that Love's own hand did make
Breathed forth the sound that said "I hate"
To me that languished for her sake;
But you, sweetest of all mine—
With eyes so bright as yours when I first looked them on--
Came down a lower pitch than my rising pride, and asked
Whereon his height came from whence he rose.
My reply was "'Tis your tongue that made both mine and thee grow."
So they kept this friendship even in hell;
And heaven to their blushing arms were beguiled
By attributes more blessed than those bosoms gave:

2
My love is as a fever, longing still
For that which longer nurseth the disease,
Feeding on that which doth preserve the ill,

That it may better be cured with new offense.
I cannot cure my learfulness of desire--
No matter how much in proof I lay;
But if within these limits mine eye beholdes,
Then 'tis sickness to take another pleasure.
O me! what can grief do but sigh? O him whom sorrow loves not!--


3
O me, what eyes hath love put in my head,
Which have no correspondence with true sight!
Or if they have, where is my judgment fled,
That viewless void which hides the heaven from peepers?

Ah know I not whither these books lend their tale,
Making holes all through myself to look,
To make a blind wonder of mine own truth.
Mine eye's perspective cannot so far penetrate
The wide and wondrous scope of your fair show;
So that it must render silent an aspect
Of poor description or praise given more dear
By others than itself surpasses its master.


4
Canst thou, O cruel, say I love thee not
When I against myself with thee partake?
Do I not think on thee when I forgot thee, dear friend;
And for my sins do base sighs begin
That bear thy shame still as mine own deserts lend. Yet in these vaults lies the treasure
Which doth more than justifies a single part of me,
Though it be but little that's most dear to thee:
The right-wise heart which is thine at home finds
At your fair appeal even then suspecting ill--

  Lest 't should seem strange and seeming contradiction
  To praise him whom you accuse so much abides.


5
O, from what power hast thou this powerful might
With insufficiency my heart to sway?
To make me give the lie to my true sight,
Or do I yield thee thy spite for truth's sake! O, be contented;
My love is as a poor father satisfied.
No more should public honor than in love
Be crowned on his worth and integrity.
So then with myself am well pleased that
Thy presence can influence minds unswaying
And bring souls nearer home where bliss lies--


6
Love is too young to know what conscience is;
Yet who knows not conscience is born of love?
Then, gentle cheater, urge not my amiss,

But make an excuse for thy crime.
Thou art but the second instance of me,
The first was thee that made this woman mine,
And I in myself return another mark.
Look on it: there's life within thine eyes!
Mine eye doth beauty still exist which loves thou more,

Since sweet forms do their birth shake and die when men are seen;
Therefore look upon them with those sad frowns they bear
When nature makes women sour flesh by changing shape.
  But since you've altered from tenderness into hate,
  Mercy so changes her manners humbly toward us.


7
In loving thee thou know'st I am forsworn,
But thou art twice forsworn, to me love swearing;
In act thy bed-vow broke, and new faith torn.
For what is this but my desert sworn?--
That in him there resides the secret of love...

And that inwardly his sweet thoughts do quake?
No, 'tis true: mine eye doth witness it so.
Thou canst not stain a pure blood against which lust reigns;
Rise up then into an ass if nature permit,


8
Cupid laid by his brand and fell asleep.
A maid of Dian's this advantage found,
And his love-kindling fire did quickly steep;
But in the deadly conflagration he grew,
The strongest hands to quench not his fierce heart:
His death is lamented everywhere,
Lives where beauty cannot revive him alone--
Nor none but fools nor vile vulgar baboons speak
Of such a conqueror's triumph.
So then I with dumb amazement behold another day!--
Him poor girl that slept on fair Rand--
Another man my friend for sport of wit --

Both so greatly amazed yet still doth remain!


9
The little love-god, lying once asleep,
Laid by his side his heart-inflaming brand,
Whilst many nymphs that vowed chaste life to keep
Bearing the heavy night's accursed burden, He drew with His hand a book
And in another line of figures painted fair flowers;
His wide pen and ink were all for show,
But this poor sleeping beauty had so much skill
That every child might read it happily.
For him straight drawing was vanity--
A dumb muse which nature should use
To make painting better than he was bred;
Which partly well done their ignorance showed,
  But chiefly Him naming them blessed knaves did rehearse.


10
When my love swears that she is made of truth,
I do believe her, though I know she lies,
That she might think me some untutored youth,
And not a learned knave o'erleaving his sword.
Then may the shame and scorn which on myself doth meet
To condemn mine own worth with such words: "O, false lover!--
Spare thee thy modesty if thou art true;
Be sharp in falsehoods to entertainers,
Not seeming for sake but pure falseness."
Thus have all my jealousies committed
Which were before resolved happily began;

  And each against another's desert pent up
A bitter hatred even unto death.


11
Two loves I have, of comfort and despair,
That like two spirits do suggest me still.
My better angel is a man right fair,
And in him my heart's sad bottom dwells;
But that same solid beauty which doth entwine his part
Hath other dear love by nature hid there.
His sweet dove lives upon the bosom o'erhead—
Who for joy attends every leaf with song,
