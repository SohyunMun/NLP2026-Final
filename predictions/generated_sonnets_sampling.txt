Those lips that Love's own hand did make
Breathed forth the sound that said "I hate"
To me that languished for her sake;
But those eyes which gave such eloquence mine,
Even then their sweet zeal deposed.
O was it not love to hear another say so?
Such tongues may breath more when they have 'translated'
Their gracious voices into harsh accents of rage!
Mine eye loved my other though quite new-made;
So in him words flowed sighs and tears describing grief.
Then spoke Rosalind: "Be gone hence immediately;"--
The raven had his call -- "'Tis thy appetite still awake,"
  Lest he should wake others sleeping with yawns.-- "No tongue but thee can speak."


1
Poor soul, the center of my sinful earth,
Pressed with these rebel powers that thee array,
Why dost thou pine within and suffer dearth,
Combinations so unmanly to be made?
What hast 't got which now must needs give away,
For nothing but means for further increase?

O me! why do I bear this burden alone,
Not witting what can fall on myself,


2
My love is as a fever, longing still
For that which longer nurseth the disease,
Feeding on that which doth preserve the ill,

That well cured friend whose dear breath doth die.

But then my sickness cannot be so unjust;--
Since nothing can save it but his pure blood,
His bones and marrow combined together to make him sick! `O, 'tis mad with wantonness,
When no passion holds such sway over it's content:


3
O me, what eyes hath love put in my head,
Which have no correspondence with true sight!
Or if they have, where is my judgment fled,

That reads the character of thy hue? O poor mine eye indeed,
What means beauty's painting without number?
Mine own sweet image doth more resemble thee
Than those painted after his fancy had it.
So thou art but twelve years old when I saw thee—
Three golden brooches on my brows dyed red,
Fourteen year before that time did make war
Between fairies and mortal men's faces;
Eighteen months since this ill event witnessed,
In heaven by earth to see him transformed go;
  And thence into hell we read o'er how he fared.


4
Canst thou, O cruel, say I love thee not
When I against myself with thee partake?
Do I not think on thee when I forgot thee, yet
My heart does swear to the contrary;
For if it were self-inflicted, 'tis far worse
Than a long sickness brought from home.
Since nothing can break hearts so sorely as ill--


5
O, from what power hast thou this powerful might
With insufficiency my heart to sway?
To make me give the lie to my true sight,
And yet desire still thy love despite of doubt!
That would be like throwing stones at a blazing fire,
Which on each stroke so valiant is destroyed.
No; 'gainst myself I throw mine eyes wide open,
Bearing aloft such angry hate that in reply answers are made
As fast as thoughts with opening their chests:
  Mine inward most deep-buried rage unlocks and opens again,
  Swooning all through apathy behind against thee.


6
Love is too young to know what conscience is;
Yet who knows not conscience is born of love?
Then, gentle cheater, urge not my amiss,

When in silence with sweet thoughts doth sit brooding,


7
In loving thee thou know'st I am forsworn,
But thou art twice forsworn, to me love swearing;
In act thy bed-vow broke, and new faith torn.
My oaths were never twofold or given twain:
Thou knew'sT fitter left than right is today;--
I in her do all my affects slander,

And that which doth affect me most calls forth My fire.
Yet canst not make myself proud of such a sin,
Although envy plagues the subject quite,
Who with him who commits this heinous crime greets,
  Finding his sweet pleasure by torturing mine!


8
Cupid laid by his brand and fell asleep.
A maid of Dian's this advantage found,
And his love-kindling fire did quickly steep
In her bosom; but nimble he was
Upon that foundation to go down again:

"Till him dry up in sleep I awoke with a cry, "No damsel can defend thee,"
Scorned because thou art female, yet strong enough;"

"'Gainst nature,' quoth she, 'thieves even so far on blood;

'Mine own rights have the strongest corner set.'

  Then what then? O meekness most horrible!
  The hardest loss is death's gain made more clear.--


9
The little love-god, lying once asleep,
Laid by his side his heart-inflaming brand,
Whilst many nymphs that vowed chaste life to keep
Played the vampiric harlot under him;
For nothing so vile in beauty's show
Hath more majesty than thy fair image. I mean thou art sweet,
And therefore 'scape not at hell for a villain--

  Lest peace should grieve me with my being told so;
  Because thee detests evil cannot be shown.


10
When my love swears that she is made of truth,
I do believe her, though I know she lies,
That she might think me some untutored youth,
And not knowing what time to pace in the park.
But then on every foul occasion when she smiles--


11
Two loves I have, of comfort and despair,
That like two spirits do suggest me still.
My better angel is a man right fair,
And for my sake he doth weep in the vain show;
For beauty's fallible form finds no delight,
It having to nature such sweet quality locked.
But where art thou that patterned this happy picture?
Who drew thy figure or whose font am I
Which inspired these wonderful accents?
Mine own love was then as summer growing wild
Or winter bearded with his golden autumn,
Beauteous bird painted all red under heaven--
Haply unseen by thee but looking forth!
